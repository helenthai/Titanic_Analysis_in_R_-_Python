The sinking of the RMS Titanic is one of the most famous shipwrecks in history. On April 15, 1912, during her maiden voyage, the Titanic sank after colliding with an iceberg, killing 1502 out of 2224 passengers and crew.

One of the reasons that the shipwreck led to such loss of life was that there were not enough lifeboats for the passengers and crew. Although there were some elements of luck involved in surviving, some groups of people were more likely to survive than others. The question is "What should be prepared to save people life?" or "What's kind of factor help people to survive in the shipwrecks besides the fact of lacking lifeboats?"

In this project, I'm digging in the provided data to find out what sorts of people were likely to survive. In other words, based on the provided data and using data analysis, I tried to find the factors that would help people to survive.

Fixing Data Types & Cleaning up data¶

Above is the data contained in a Pandas DataFrame. Skipping through the whole table, we can see a bit of information.

First, it lets us know we have 891 observations, or passengers, to analyze here:

891 entries, 0 to 890

Next it shows us all of the columns and each of them holds information of the observations, such as the name, sex or age. These colunms let us know how many values each of them contains. While most of the columns have complete data on every observation, like the survived feature here:

survived    891  non-null values

some are missing information, like the age column:

age         714  non-null values

These missing values are represented as NaNs (Not A Number). To have a clean dataset, we will remove the NaN values from every columns.

Last but not least, the columns "ticket" and "cabin" have many missing values and the datatype is inconsistent, so they can’t add much value to the analysis. To handle this we will drop them from the dataframe to preserve the integrity of the dataset. The columns "fare" also doesn't give much information to analyze in the relationship with survival rate because it could be covered by the traveling class (pclass). The higher class might have bought the higher fare price. So I also decide to drop it.

Conclusion¶

With my assumptions:

  • that there were not enough lifeboats and lifebuoys for all people is not the main survival factor
  • that all the variables I analyzed are the features listed in the dataset
  • that the factors like sickness or disability assumely did not effect to survival capacity

In conclusion,we could say that the factors of gender, age, and traveling class are the most possible contribution to the probability of survival after analysing this dataset. The overall survival rate for men was 20%. For women, it was 75%. The low class women were more likely to survive than the high class men. And the high class men were twice as likely to survive as low class men. Traveling class is a far weaker variable in determining survival rate than sex or age. Indeed, most of the variance in high class vs. low class survival rates can be attributed to sex and gender. Because the survival rate for women was far greater than the survival rate for men, we would thus expect a much higher survival rate for the high class passengers as a whole than for the low class passengers. There were one more factor to increase the survival chance is traveling with family. The best size is travelling with 1,2 or 3 people.

On the other hand, in my analysis, there are a few limitations I would like to discuss. First of all, I want to emphazise that the dataset is a sample of the population; it covered 1/3 of the population (712 over about 2224 passengers and crew). However, this sample is not a normal distritution, that's why I can not use z-test or t-test to test my assumptions. Secondly, to explore the survival factors, it contained reluctant data; fare price was an example. The fare price had a large range from 0 to 512 USD and did not show any consistent relationship to survival rate. The dataset also contained "nosing" data such as "ticket" and "cabin". As detailed explantation, are the feature "ticket" and "cabin" hav many missing values and the datatype is inconsistent. My solution to handle this limitation is that I dropped the unnecessary and no-value data from the dataframe to preserve the integrity of the dataset. I dropped the feature of ticket, cabin, fare price from the dataframe. For the features of Age which held many NaN values, I also decided to remove all the NaN rows to polish the data and make the statistical analysis more accurate. That's why the dataset decreased from 890 entries to 712 entries. I believe that cleaning up data is one of the crucial task in analysis. The preciseness depends much on a good data.
